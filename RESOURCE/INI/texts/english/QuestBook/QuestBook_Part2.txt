Tutorial_Fra {Reference: What’s happening in France?}
Tutorial_Fra.1
{
On March 10, 1661, Cardinal Mazarin died, and the young King Louis XIV assumed full power. In a very short time, Louis brought order to the state; he took an interest in everything. He was a ruler who wanted to “know and be able to do everything”: the king asked questions of his ministers to better understand state affairs, of the president of parliament to learn how to govern, of judges so as not to miss any detail, and of ladies so as not to fall behind in the arts of gallantry. Truly, France was fortunate with its monarch.

All the more so because this was not just a king, but the Sun King, surrounded by the most refined veneration compared to other European monarchs. The Aztec calendar system culminated in the so-called cycles, each of which lasted four times thirteen years, that is, 52 years. At the end of such a cycle, as we know from Mexica mythology, the Aztecs always expected a great, destructive natural catastrophe. Awaiting the end of the "old cycle" (the last time it was marked was in 1507), the inhabitants of Tenochtitlan gathered on a hill that became known as the Star Mountain. The end of the cycle came when the star Aldebaran (according to another version, a star from the Pleiades constellation) reached its zenith. The ruler would then solemnly kindle a new fire, and throughout the country, throughout Mexico, a grand celebration was held in honor of the New Fire—a festival marking the happy end of the old calendar cycle and the beginning of a new one. Fires were lit everywhere again, pregnant women and children could leave their homes, and artisans began making new pottery.

Now let us briefly touch on Aztec literature, as well as the question of how and on what the Mexica wrote. They wrote either on prepared deerskin or on Aztec paper made from maguey (agave). The sheets of codices made from such paper were glued together; in this way, Mexica scribes, who were rightly considered artists, created something akin to our accordion-fold books. Aztec writing was pictographic, that is, it consisted of individual sign-drawings, and the combination of several such signs could denote a word or concept with a meaning quite different from the same signs taken separately. The Aztecs borrowed pictographic writing from the Mixtecs. From them, they also adopted numbers. The number one was depicted as a finger, but sometimes all numbers from one to twenty were simply indicated by dots or dashes. Twenty was represented by a flag. The last number of the second order (202) was depicted as a burning tree (meaning "as many as the hairs on a head"). Finally, to indicate 203, that is, 8,000, the Aztecs drew a sack (meaning "as countless as the cocoa beans in a sack").

The Aztecs created an astonishingly rich, extraordinarily mature literature. Its best expert, the Mexican Americanist Angel Maria Garibay, names a whole range of literary genres known to the Mexica: didactic treatises, dramatic works, prose. However, all these literary genres played only a secondary role among the Aztecs. By literature, the Aztecs, like other Nahuatl-speaking and writing peoples of pre-Columbian Mexico, mainly meant poetry. If we look in a Nahuatl dictionary, we see that the word "cuicani" has two meanings—poet and singer. From this, we can conclude that Aztec poetry was intended primarily for public performance. The works of poet-singers, which found a particularly wide response in Aztec society, were passed down from generation to generation through oral tradition. In such poem-songs, the cuicani often depicted themselves...

Look, it is I, the singer, composing verses, shining like precious jades, shimmering like the waves of the sea, I command my voice, I command the harmony of singing flutes and the ringing of bells, the ringing of golden bells. I sing my fragrant song, like multicolored ornaments, a song sparkling with gems, shimmering with jade, I sing my hymn to blossoming spring.

It is appropriate to recall here that the extraordinary development of artistic literature in ancient Mexico was primarily facilitated by the common language of the Mexica tribes—Nahuatl, whose dialects differ only slightly from each other. The wide spread of a unified literary form of the Nahuatl language and the nobility of its classical form were owed by the Aztecs to a woman for whom Tenochtitlan was not even her homeland. This was at a time when the Aztecs did not yet control all of Mexico. In those days, the powerful and educated ruler of the major city-state of Texcoco, named Techotlala (1357–1409), married the daughter of the ruler of the city of Culhuacan and adopted from her the Culhuacan dialect of Nahuatl. Thanks to his influence, this language then gained dominance throughout Mexico. Special credit here belongs to one of the most significant figures in the history of indigenous America—the ruler of Texcoco, Nezahualcoyotl (1418–1472).

Architecture occupied a unique but extremely important place among Aztec arts. Few of the monumental creations of Aztec architects have survived: the foundations of temples in Mexico City, city walls in Huexotla, the round temple in Calixtlahuaca, and finally, the recently discovered temple in Malinalco. This entire temple, shaped like a traditional Aztec pyramid, was carved directly into the rock. Its construction undoubtedly required enormous physical effort from workers who used only stone tools.

A brilliant achievement of Aztec architects and city planners was the magnificent imperial capital of Tenochtitlan, the largest city in the Americas at the time, and probably the largest in the world then. On November 8, 1519, when Cortés entered this beautiful city with his soldiers for the first time, Tenochtitlan had about 650,000 inhabitants.

When Cortés entered Tenochtitlan, he was struck by the fantastic sight. Apparently, for this reason, the conquistadors later tried to leave no trace of Tenochtitlan, so their victory would seem more convincing. Fortunately, one of Cortés’s soldiers, Bernal Díaz, described everything he saw. Thanks to his account, the accuracy of which has been confirmed by major archaeological excavations, we now know what this largest city of the time was like.

Tenochtitlan rose in the middle of a salt lake. It was located on interconnected islands, and some of its buildings stood on stilts. The city was connected to the mainland by three causeways, corresponding to the number of city gates. One led to Tepeyac, another to Tlacopan, and the third to the beautiful coastal city of Chapultepec. Tenochtitlan was crossed not only by streets but also by canals, which led some historians to compare Tenochtitlan to Venice. The pride of the city, an expression of the power of the Aztecs and their rulers, was the palace of one of Montezuma II’s predecessors, Axayacatl, and even more so the palace of Montezuma II himself. From the accounts of members of the Spanish expedition, to whom Montezuma offered accommodation in Axayacatl’s palace, we know what this palace looked like. It consisted of several dozen single-story stone buildings, so that all seven thousand members of Cortés’s expedition could be comfortably housed there. Montezuma’s palace was even more magnificent. Its highlight was a kind of zoological garden, which housed many thousands of birds of the most colorful plumage and quite a few animals. Three hundred servants cared for Montezuma’s birds alone; snakes, the special object of Aztec worship, were kept in wooden cages lined with the bright feathers of rare birds. Alongside exotic birds and animals, Montezuma’s palace also “collected” exotic people: dwarfs and all sorts of oddities. The status of Montezuma’s subjects is illustrated by this fact: many parents deliberately deformed their children to ensure them a lifelong livelihood in this sad “anthropological” museum.

Even the feudal splendor of the royal court in Madrid paled in the eyes of the Spaniards when they saw the wealth and luxury of the powerful Montezuma’s palace. Alongside these three main gods, the Aztecs also greatly revered various gods and goddesses of harvest, fertility, rain, patrons of individual plants, and so on. Among them, above all, was the god Tlaloc—or rather, the multitude of Tlalocs. Tlaloc, the god of rain, was worshipped throughout the Meseta long before the arrival of the Mexica, and each community also had its own “local” Tlaloc. The wife of the main Tlaloc was the goddess of rivers and lakes, Chalchiuhtlicue. She was depicted as a young, charming woman. Tlaloc’s sister was the goddess of the harvest, Chicomecoatl (Seven Serpents). The goddess of flowers was Xochiquetzal, who was also considered the patroness of artisans. The goddess of agave was Mayahuel, and her four hundred divine sons were patrons of the liquor—octli. Another god of liquor was Tepoztecatl, who was also the supreme tribal god in Tepoztlan. The deity who was originally the patron of agriculture was probably also the Aztec god of human sacrifice, Xipe Totec.

The religious rituals of the Aztecs are also quite interesting. The most important part of the Aztec ritual was sacrifice, and the highest offering was considered to be human blood, for according to the Mexica’s religious beliefs, it was the “food of the gods.” In Aztec codices, the sign for “blood” was sometimes replaced by the sign for “precious stone” or the sign for “flower.” The Aztecs considered it their holiest duty to provide the gods with this food—sacred blood, which possessed special magical power. It could be offered to the gods drop by drop, but a ritual killing was considered an incomparably more effective sacrifice. The intended victim was laid on a round sacrificial stone, after which the chief officiant would cut open the living victim’s chest with an obsidian knife, tear out the heart, and sprinkle the altar with this bleeding, still-beating heart. When the Spaniards from Cortés’s expedition entered the main temple of Tenochtitlan, they counted, according to their accounts, 130,000 human skulls there. All these people had been sacrificed in just a few years before the arrival of the Spaniards. Religious fanaticism was one of the main motives for Aztec conquests. In battle, the Aztecs by no means aimed to kill the enemy. In their eyes, a captured enemy was much more valuable—he could be offered as a sacrifice to the gods.

Sometimes the Aztecs themselves voluntarily offered themselves as sacrifices to the gods, in order to attain “bliss in the afterlife.” People chosen by the priests for sacrifice were treated with great honor, especially those destined for sacrifice to Tezcatlipoca. A year before the sacrifice, the most handsome and noble among the prisoners of war was chosen, and for the entire year, eight priests taught him the commanding and majestic manners of a ruler. Everyone around paid him divine honors. Accompanied by priests, he would walk through Tenochtitlan, playing the flute, and people would greet him respectfully. A month before the sacrifice, four of the most beautiful girls were chosen for him, who were obliged to fulfill his every wish. Then came the day of death. Accompanied by a solemn procession, the doomed man walked to the temple. There he said farewell to his wives, and the eight priests who had cared for him all year slowly led him up the wide staircase. On each step of the temple, he broke a sacred flute. And now he was at the top. There the priests seized him, and the high priest tore the heart from his chest... But only the most noble prisoners of war were honored with such a solemn killing. Ordinary prisoners were sacrificed by the hundreds during less formal local ceremonies.

Sometimes, a method was used in sacrifices that the Spaniards later called “gladiatorial.” The prisoner was tied to a heavy stone, but left enough room to move. The priests gave him wooden weapons, and the captive had to fight several better-armed Aztec warriors, whose movements, of course, were not restricted in any way.

Children and women were sacrificed less often. Women were sacrificed in honor of the earth goddess (before the killing, to forget their cruel fate, they would dance for several hours in a row until they fell into a state of ecstasy). Children were sacrificed in honor of the god of fertility, Tlaloc. There are also some unclear accounts of other Aztec sacrificial rituals. For example, in honor of the fire god Huehueteotl, bound prisoners of war were supposedly thrown into the fire and slowly burned.

BOARDING – to come alongside, to bring a ship side by side with another. To board – to storm and seize a ship in hand-to-hand combat.
*
BACKSTAY WIND – a following slanting wind, between a running wind (before the wind) and a beam wind (half-wind), and also the course, route, or heading of a ship with this wind, which is why a ship sails on a starboard or port tack, depending on whether the wind is coming from the right or left side.
*
FORECASTLE (Dutch bak) – a superstructure at the bow of the deck, reaching to the stem. Previously, the forecastle referred to the forward part of the upper deck (in front of the foremast). It serves to protect the upper deck from being flooded by oncoming waves, to increase buoyancy, to house service rooms, etc.
*
BARQUE – a three- or four-masted sea vessel with square sails on the forward masts and fore-and-aft sails on the aft (mizzen) mast.
*
CLOSE-HAULED – a sailing course where the ship’s longitudinal axis forms an angle of less than 90 degrees with the wind direction. To sail close-hauled means to sail almost against the wind, coming slightly from the right or left of the bow.
*
MIZZENMAST (from Dutch mast) – a vertical metal or wooden structure on the deck. The aft mast, following the mainmast, is called the mizzenmast.
*
FIRE SHIP – a vessel loaded with combustible and explosive materials; in the age of sail, it was used to set enemy ships on fire.
*
BRIG – a two-masted sailing vessel with square rigging, essentially a two-masted, smaller frigate.
*
BRIGANTINE – a two-masted vessel with square sails on the foremast and fore-and-aft sails on the aft mast.
*
BUCCANEERS (French boucaniers), cattle herders, many of whom later became privateers, based on the islands of the West Indies. They attacked Spanish colonies in America, as well as Spanish and other merchant ships.
*
BOWSPRIT – an inclined, round wooden spar projecting forward from the bow of a ship, with one end fixed to the vessel. Together with its extension, the jibboom, it serves to secure the lower ends of stays that support the foremast and its topmasts from the front.
*
SHIPYARD (from Dutch werf) – an enterprise for building ships.
*
DISPLACEMENT – the amount of water displaced by a floating vessel; a characteristic of a ship’s size. There is mass displacement, equal to the mass of the vessel, i.e., the mass of water contained in the volume of the ship’s submerged part, and volumetric displacement (the volume of the ship below the waterline).
*
GALLEON – a large, three-masted ship of particularly strong construction, equipped with heavy artillery. These ships were used to transport goods from Spanish and Portuguese colonies.
*
GALIOT – a flat-bottomed Dutch vessel for coastal (cabotage) navigation.
*
TACK (Dutch hals) – the course of a vessel relative to the wind (for example, a ship is on a starboard tack when the wind is blowing on the starboard side).
*
BEAM REACH (Dutch halfwind, literally “half-wind”) – a sailing course where the ship’s longitudinal axis is perpendicular to the wind direction.
*
GAFF (from Dutch gaffel, literally “forks”) – an inclined spar, attached at its lower end to the upper part of a mast. It is used for hoisting flags and signals; on sailing ships, the upper edge of a fore-and-aft sail is attached to the gaff.
*
MAINMAST – the second mast from the bow of the ship.
*
CABLE LENGTH – a nautical unit of length (185.2 meters), one-tenth of a nautical mile.
*
CABOTAGE (French cabotage) – navigation between ports of the same country. There is long cabotage (between ports of different seas, e.g., the Baltic and Black Seas) and short cabotage (between ports of the same or two adjacent seas, e.g., the Black and Azov Seas).
*
COASTAL NAVIGATION – sailing a vessel along the coast.
*
PRIVATEERING (from Dutch kaper – sea robber) – attacks by armed private merchant ships of a belligerent state, authorized by so-called letters of marque, on enemy merchant ships or neutral ships carrying cargo for the enemy state. Prohibited by the Paris Declaration Respecting Maritime Law of 1856.
*
QUARTERDECK (English quarter-deck, quarter – quarter, deck – deck) – a raised part of the upper deck at the stern of a ship
